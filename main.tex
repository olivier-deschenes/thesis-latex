\section{Data}
\label{sec:data}

The data used in this thesis comes from three independent sources, each addressing a distinct aspect of the problem: the spatial distribution of Bergen's population, the parcel demand they generate, and the pedestrian network connecting them to possible candidate ports. Population data is provided at the household level by the Bergen municipality for the year 2020. Parcel demand is based on PostNord's weekly volumes by postal code of 2020. Finnaly, the walking network of Bergen is extracted from \ac{osm}. The remainder of this chapter describes these sources in detail and the steps taken to prepare them for use in the models that follow.

\subsection{Walking network of Bergen}
\label{subsec:walking_bergen}

\ac{osm} is first used to retreive the walking graph of Bergen by using the Python package \texttt{OSMnx} \parencite{osmnx} using its \texttt{graph\_from\_place()} function. This package offers the ability to retreive the graph of a specific place (\texttt{place\_name="Bergen, Norway"}) and network type (\texttt{network\_type="walk"}). However, during stop checking of the walking distance, it was detected that the network type filter was too narrow as the Google Map dap distances were generally smaller than the calculated distance based on the graph. After investigation, the network filter exluded all bycible lanes, even if they were labed as walkable by \ac{osm}. The filter was therefore changed to allow byciable lanes if they are walkable.

\subsection{Population and Household Data}
\label{subsec:population-household-data}

The household data was obtained from the Master' thesis of \cite{pop_thesis} and originaly comes from the Bergen municipality. The dataset, from 2020, has information about population of the city at the household level, only the coordinates, number of resident, postal code and district was kept from the original data. A total of \num{149308} household was contained in the dataset (\num{73393} being unique), with around \num{280491} total population. This demographic is splitted into Bergen' eight districts, and \num{142} unique postal codes.

To reduce the overall values of our data, the household coordinate were snapped to the nearest \ac{osm} node from our walking network. This helped us reduced drasticly the amount of rows for our dataset to \num{29991} unique \ac{osm} nodes. During this aggregation, some \ac{osm} nodes were assigned population that were from multiple differents districts, representing around \num{418} people (\qty{0.15}{\percent} of the population) and \num{27} nodes (\qty{0.07}{\percent} of nodes). Due to some results being shown per district, these nodes were excluded. The population distribution by districts can be seen in \Cref{fig:district_population}. It should be noted that some districts will be filtered out of the population data in Section \ref{subsec:temp} (\nameref{subsec:temp}).

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.8\textwidth]{graphs/district_population.pdf}
  \caption{Total population of Bergen in 2020 aggregated by district.}
  \label{fig:district_population}
\end{figure}

Since the goal of this data is to identify good port candidates based on population coverage rather than absolute population size, the data will not be updated to current figures. Only the spatial distribution will be taken into account, and it is assumed that the population growth has been geographically uniform.

\subsection{Distance and Travel Time Data}
\label{subsec:distance-travel-time-data}

Having defined our household nodes and ports nodes datasets, we can now build our distance matrix. To acheive this, the Python package \texttt{networkx} \parencite{networkx} was used to calculate the shortes distance between all combination of port and household nodes using its \texttt{single\_source\_dijkstra\_path\_length()} function. Resulted in a $m \times n$ matrix $D$. With $d_{i,j}$ representing the Dijkstra shortest-path distance between the $i$-th port and the $j$-th household nodes\footnote{All pairs were reachable by walking due to the \ac{osm} node snapping and filtering of the port nodes.}. For this thesis, the distance between any $port_i$ and $household_j$ is assumed to be symmetric ($d_{i,j} = d_{j,i}$).

To calculate walking time, an average walking speed first needed to be defined. Based on the work of \cite{walkingspeed}, which reviewed numerous walking speed studies worldwide, a walking speed of \qty{1.25}{m/s} was identified as an appropriate general assumption. However, in the absence of studies specifically focused on Bergen, walking speed may differ from this general assumption and could be significantly affected by seasonal, weather, and topographical conditions, particularly elevation changes along walking paths. As the impact of these factors on walking speed is difficult to quantify specifically, the general assumption will be retained for this thesis. The resulted population coverage by walking time can be seen in Figure \ref{fig:population_coverage} when all candidate port nodes are available.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.8\textwidth]{graphs/walking_time_coverage.pdf}
  \caption{Percentage of the population covered by walking time with all ports available.}
  \label{fig:population_coverage}
\end{figure}

\subsection{PostNord Parcel Demand Data}
\label{subsec:postnord-parcel-demand-data}

Parcel demand data was obtained from \cite{household}, but originated from PostNord. It covers weekly (from week \num{1} to \num{48}) shipment and parcel volumes by postal code for the year 2020. The dataset had demand for \num{439} unique postal codes. The given dataset contained information about the number of shipment and packages, however, in many cases, the amount of shipment was larger than the amount of packages for row. The demand was therefore calculated as the minimum of both colums.

As compared to the household data, the real demand size is important to optimize our ports capacity. The PostNord dataset being from 2020, it needs to be adjusted to reflect 2026 levels. This was acheived by using PostNord's annual reports, wich gives the total amount of parcel deliveries for the report's year. The yearly PostNord's demand can be found in Table \ref{anx:yearly_pn_demand} in the \nameref{sec:appendix}. Based on these yearly demands, the 2026 Bergen demand can be derived using the \ac{cagr} formula. Due to the Covid-19 pandemic, only the years 2022 to 2025 are used to calculate the growth rate, starting from a year in which demand had begun to normalize after the pandemic \parencite{pitney_bowes_index_2023}. As shown in Equation~\ref{eq:postnord_cagr}, the rate is \qty{4.68}{\percent} per year. Therefore, we can approximate that the 2026 demand has grew by around \qty{48.56}{\percent} since 2020.

Second, as the dataset represents only PostNord' share of the market and the year 2020, it needs to be adjusted to the current market figures. Using the \textit{Report on the Use of Postal Services by Private Individuals and Businesses in Norway, 2025}\footnote{Translated from \textit{Rapport om privatpersoners og bedrifters bruk av posttjenester i Norge - 2025} in norwegian.} \parencite{nkom_post_2025} done by the Norwegian Communications Authority, while not provide exact volume-based market shares for parcel deliveries, surveys consumer usage patterns across Norway's primary delivery companies. The market share of PostNord was then derived from the normalized results of the private market parcel survey to be around \qty{20}{\percent}. The survey data can be found in Table \ref{anx:private_parcel_usage} in the \nameref{sec:appendix}.

Third, this dataset represents parcel demand for both households and other types of clients. However, since this thesis focuses on populations, the non-household demand needs to be removed. To achieve this, the household postcode data was joined with the weekly demand, and postcodes not present in the household dataset were excluded.

Finally, since some districts will be filtered out of the population data in Section \ref{subsec:temp} (\nameref{subsec:temp}), the demand associated with these excluded household districts will also be removed. The overall demand quantities and changes by step can be seen in Table \ref{anx:parcel_volumes} in the \nameref{sec:appendix}. The total parcel demand taken in considuration for this Thesis will be \num{5977449} parcel for the year 2026.

\subsection{Disaggregating Weekly Demand into Daily Demand}
\label{subsec:weekly-to_daily-demand}

Since daily scheduling for the boat network requires daily demand for each selected port, the PostNord weekly demand needs to be broken down. While several approaches were possible, such as splitting the demand equally across days or distributing it randomly, applying specific daily rates was chosen as the most representative of real demand patterns. Based on the work of \cite{dailydemand}, where a daily operational parcels operations was studied, the 5 Day split for this Thesis context can therefore be deduce by adding the weekend's demand to Monday. The resulted daily rates can be seen in Table \ref{anx:day_shares} in the \nameref{sec:appendix}.

\subsection{Disaggregating Postcal Code Demand into Household Demand}
\label{subsec:postal-code-to-household-demand}

To correcly assinged demands to the nearest chosen ports, the demand of each Households must be known. However, the PostNord dataset is only by postal code. Nevertheless, the demand can still be allocated to each household based on its share of the population within its postal code. For example, if a postal code has a weekly demand of 100 parcels and contains two households, one with 1 resident ($a$) and the other with 2 ($b$), the demand is distributed as $\frac{1}{3}$ to $a$ and $\frac{2}{3}$ to $b$. In addition, each household demand was rounded to have integer demand, resulting in a loss of \num{15.69} total parcels or \qty{0.000263}{\percent} of total demand.

\subsection{Depot Location}
\label{sub:depot_loc}

With the candidate ports and their demand defined, the depot location can now be chosen. The depot is where the incoming parcels are received, processed and loaded onto the boats, so it needs enough space for this work and for docking the boats. However, these processing and handling spaces will be taken into account as context, but not quantified in this thesis. Furthermore, a good depot location should be close to the Port of Bergen and its cargo rail station, as both are the second largest of their kind in Norway \parencite{bergenport,bergenrail}. Finally, the depot also acts as a port itself, so a location with a high population nearby is the most efficient choice, as it removes the most parcels from boat transport.

The first step consists of identifying the locations of the Port of Bergen and the railway station, and overlaying them with household density. As shown in Figure~\ref{fig:full_density}, the two facilities are spatially separated. The port is located along the shoreline, whereas the railway station is situated further inland. Since the depot also needs to be close to the shore, the range of potential locations becomes considerably restricted. A full map density can be fun in Figure \ref{anx:map_density} of the \nameref{sec:appendix}.

Additionally, it offers some empty space near the shore. As explained earlier, this space may not be actually usable for this project, but as for the candidate ports, it will be assumed that all locations offers enought space and could be used to build on.

\subsection{Data Limitations}
\label{subsec:data-limitations}

Although a substantial amount of data is available to address the thesis's research problem, some limitations remain: part of the required information is missing, and other elements must be approximated. The following subsections discuss each of these limitations in turn.

\subsubsection{Lack of data}

The first data that is missing is the information about the weight and volumn of the parcels, only the demand is present. Even if some average volumn and weight was chosen for the demand, the boat transporting the packages does not have any real volumn or weight limits, only a capacity was chosen. Therefore, these two variables will be ignored. The second data that is missing is related to the ports candiate real hability to be converted to a port location. Indeed, some ports could be not suitable for this operation or be privite and in no way of having a locker system be build on it.

\subsubsection{Approximation}

As described in the previous subsections, some data had to be approximated since exact figures were not available. First, the weekly demand was disaggregated into daily demand, and then further broken down to the household level. The resulting demand was subsequently scaled to the current year and finally adjusted to reflect the total market share of all shipping companies. The thesis could have directly used the 2020 data, but this would have produced an outdated scenario for waterway parcel deliveries, since demand has grown significantly since then.

Furthermore, walking time is directly computed from the walking distance and does not account for elevation changes. This simplification is consistent with common practice in pedestrian network analysis, as similar frameworks do not dynamically account for topographic slopes \parencite{noslope}. While elevation can have a significant impact on pedestrian routing, especially in hilly cities \parencite{slopegood}, incorporating slope-based travel time falls outside the scope of this thesis as it would need to take into account the total affect on the round trip to the port and is identified as a direction for future work.

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:methodology}

In this section, the methodology of this thesis is explained in detail, in four separate parts. First, the model used to select a set of ports based on a desired walking distance is presented. Second, the two models used to generate a set of routes for these ports are described. Finally, it is explained how, given a set of ports and routes, a schedule can be constructed to measure the KPIs based on the yearly demand.

\subsection{Port Selection Model}
\label{subsec:port_selection_model}

The goal of this stage is to find the smallest set of ports that still covers a target share of the population within an acceptable walking time. The selection depends on two inputs: the walking threshold and the target coverage percentage. These two configurations are presented first, followed by the optimisation model chosen to perform the selection.

\subsubsection{Walking Thresholds}
\label{ssec:walking_time}

\begin{figure}[httbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=1\textwidth]{graphs/walking_time_coverage_district.pdf}
  \caption{Percentage of the population covered by walking time with all ports available by district.}
  \label{fig:district_coverage}
\end{figure}

As discussed in the background section, a walking time of \qty{15}{minutes} is often the new goal when planning new city programs for its population. The walking time coverage distribution was alreay shown in Figure \ref{fig:population_coverage}, but it can be important to show also see the same data by district. This can be visualize in Figure \ref{fig:district_coverage}.

% ['Arna', 'Bergenhus', 'Fana', 'Fylingsdalen', 'Laksevåg', 'Ytrebygda', 'Årstad', 'Åsane']

The districts can be visually grouped into three categories based on how their population coverage evolves with the walking time threshold. The first group, composed of Bergenhus ({\color[HTML]{FF7500}\rule{1.5ex}{1.5ex}}), Ytrebygda ({\color[HTML]{7F7F7F}\rule{1.5ex}{1.5ex}}), and Laksevåg ({\color[HTML]{955348}\rule{1.5ex}{1.5ex}}), shows a rapid increase in coverage, reaching near-full coverage within approximately \qtyrange{35}{40}{\minute} of walking. The third group, formed by Arna ({\color[HTML]{0079B9}\rule{1.5ex}{1.5ex}}), Fana ({\color[HTML]{00A300}\rule{1.5ex}{1.5ex}}), and Åsane ({\color[HTML]{00C1D2}\rule{1.5ex}{1.5ex}}), are the opposite: coverage grows slowly and does not reach \qty{90}{\percent} even at a one-hour walking threshold, plateauing between \qtyrange{75}{85}{\percent}. The second group, composed of Årstad ({\color[HTML]{BCBD00}\rule{1.5ex}{1.5ex}}) and Fyllingsdalen ({\color[HTML]{9C65C2}\rule{1.5ex}{1.5ex}}), follows an intermediate pattern: their coverage initially grows more slowly than in the first group, but eventually reaches \qty{100}{\percent} at higher walking thresholds, around \qtyrange{50}{55}{\minute}. With these remarks, it was decided to exclude Arna, Fana and Åsane from the thesis as our port location can not reach them in a realistic time, combine with the points talked in the backtround section.

The walking time threshold considered for this problem ranges from \qtyrange{15}{30}{\minute}, with every integer value within this range tested. Values below \qty{15}{\minute} were judged as too low for this context, as the resulting coverage was very limited, while values above \qty{30}{\minute} were judged as impractical, since citizens are unlikely to walk such distances to collect a parcel.

\subsubsection{Coverage Percentages}
\label{ssec:coverage_per}

As achieving \qty{100}{\percent} coverage for a given walking time threshold could require a disproportionately large number of boats, due to certain households only being reachable through specific ports, a minimum coverage percentage will instead be defined to address this issue. It was decided that the range would be \qtyrange{75}{100}{\percent}, with every percentage point within this range tested. This range should be large enough to show if some ports are added only for a small portion of the population.

\subsubsection{Partial Set Covering Location Problem}
\label{ssec:pscl_problem}

Although various models exist to address this routing problem, the \ac{psclp} was selected for this thesis. It perfectly matches the network's operational objectives as it minimizes the number of active ports while relaxing the requirement to reach all households \parencite{psclp}.

Table \ref{tab:pslcp_configs} presents the model configurations, all combinations of which were tested.

Table \ref{tab:psclp_notation} details the notation used in the model. The changes made in the notation were mostly done to contextualize it for the thesis, as we have household and port nodes instead of demand nodes and facilities, while using population instead of weight. The \textit{Data and Sets} section of the table describes what the model needs as input, with the set of port and household nodes and population for each households. The reachable household nodes $J$ is calculated using the previously defined distance matrix $M$, where every household nodes whitout a port node close enough is removed from considuration. The maximum distance used is derived from the walking speed and the current run $T_{max}$ resulted in a maximum amount of meters to given to reach a possible port.

  \end{tabularx}
\end{table}

Building upon the established notation, the problem is formulated as an optimization model as follows:

\begin{align}
  \min \quad & \sum_{i \in I} f_i y_i \label{eq:psclp-objective}                                     \\
  \text{s.t.} \quad
             & \sum_{i \in I(j)} y_i \geq z_j
             &                                                   & j \in J \label{eq:psclp-coverage} \\
             & \sum_{j \in J} d_j z_j \geq D
  \label{eq:psclp-demand}                                                                            \\
             & y_i \in \{0,1\}
             &                                                   & i \in I \label{eq:psclp-y-binary} \\
             & z_j \in \{0,1\}
             &                                                   & j \in J \label{eq:psclp-z-binary}
\end{align}

The objective function~\eqref{eq:psclp-objective} traditionally minimizes the total fixed cost of the opened ports. Since every candidate port is given the same opening cost ($f_i = 1$), this is the same as minimizing the number of ports selected for the network.

Constraint~\eqref{eq:psclp-coverage} ensures that for a household node $j$ to be covered ($z_j = 1$), at least one port from its subset $I(j)$ must be opened. If no port able to reach $j$ within the walking threshold $T_{\max}$ is opened, $z_j$ is forced to bo not covered ($z_j = 0$).

Constraint~\eqref{eq:psclp-demand} ensures that the covered set of household populations is greater or equal to our minum covered population $D$.

The last two constraints,~\eqref{eq:psclp-y-binary} and~\eqref{eq:psclp-z-binary}, keep the port and coverage variables binary.

\subsubsection{Model implementation}
\label{ssec:psclp_implementation}

The \ac{psclp} is implemented with Google OR-Tools \parencite{ortools}, using its constraint-programming solver CP-SAT \parencite{cpsat}. One adjustment was needed to fit the model to the solver. CP-SAT only works with integer coefficients, but the coverage target $D = \alpha \sum_{j \in J} d_j$ is usually not a whole number, since $\alpha$ is a fraction. To account for this, the population and the coverage target are multiplied by \num{1000} and rounded to integers before the demand constraint~\eqref{eq:psclp-demand} is built. This keeps three decimal places of precision, so the rounding has no real effect on the result.

The model is kept as compact as possible by removing, before optimization, all households that cannot be covered by any candidate port within the walking threshold $T_{\max}$. These households are therefore excluded from the formulation, and no associated coverage variable $z_j$ is introduced.

\subsubsection{Demand Allocation to Selected Ports}
\label{ssec:demand_allocation}

The \ac{psclp} gives a set of selected ports, but it does not say the households each port should serve. Each household is assigned to the nearest selected port based on the distance matrix $M$. The total household demands is then summed by ports to get a weekly demand for each ports.

It should be noted that this is done for every household, not only for the ones the \ac{psclp} counted as covered. A household that was left uncovered, meaning that no selected port can be reached within the walking threshold $T_{\max}$, is still assigned to its nearest selected port, only with a longer walk. The selected ports therefore receive the parcel demand of every household in the included districts, and the coverage threshold $\alpha$ only shapes which ports are selected and how far people have to walk, not the amount of demand the network has to carry.

\subsubsection{Operational Constraints}
\label{ssec:operational_constraints}

Operating a waterway parcel delivery service has multiple logicsital and operations constrains, this thesis will only take into account the following:

\begin{description}
  \item[Maximum Capacity:] This thesis uses the Candela P-12 Shuttle, an electric hydrofoil ferry, as its reference boat \parencite{candela2026p12}. Since this boat is built to carry passengers and not parcels, its capacity first has to be converted into a parcel capacity. This is done by weight: the boat holds \num{29} passengers, and with an average weight of \qty{75}{\kilogram} per passenger \parencite{nordicboatstandard}, this gives a payload of \qty{2175}{\kilogram}. Dividing this payload by the average parcel weight of \qty{4.8}{\kilogram} \parencite{parcelweight} gives a baseline capacity of about \num{450} parcels. As the real usable capacity of such a service is uncertain, the capacity is treated as a parameter and tested from this baseline up to \num{3000} parcels, the larger values standing for a bigger conventional boat rather than the Candela P-12.

  \item[Service Speed:] The service speed sets how fast the boat travels between ports. Being a hydrofoil, the Candela P-12 can reach around \num{20} knots, but it stays light and is therefore limited in capacity, while a larger conventional boat carries more parcels but is slower. Capacity and speed are therefore tested together as pairs: about \num{450} parcels at \num{20} knots, or \numrange{1500}{3000} parcels at \num{8} knots. In addition, Bergen's waterways have their own speed limits, and parts of every crossing fall inside zones restricted to \num{8} or \num{5} knots. The travel time between two ports is computed from the nautical distance of the crossing, with each stretch covered at its matching speed: \num{8} or \num{5} knots in the restricted zones, and the service speed in open water.

  \item[Crew Size:] Each boat is operated by a crew. The crew is responsible for discharging the parcels at the ports, and since the employees discharge parcels in parallel, the discharge time at a port is divided by the number of employees on board. A larger crew therefore lowers the time spent at each port, but it also raises the number of employees the service needs. For this reason, the crew size is also treated as a parameter and tested with \num{3} and \num{4} employees per boat.

  \item[Maneuvering Time:] Every time the boat travels from one port to another, it needs time to dock and undock. This thesis accounts for it with a fixed maneuvering time, added to the travel time of every trip between two ports. The value used is a baseline of \qty{3.5}{\minute}, taken as the average of two estimates: a conservative one of about \qty{5}{\minute}, derived from a study of port operations on a passenger ferry \parencite{klovning2024efficiency}, and an optimistic one of \qty{2}{\minute}, based on the Candela P-12 \parencite{candela2026p12}.

  \item[Maximum Route Duration:] A single boat route, from the moment it leaves the depot to the moment it returns, cannot last longer than \qty{8}{\hour}. This upper bound represents a normal working shift for the crew. The duration of a route is the sum of all its travel times, including the maneuvering time, and the discharge time at every port it visits. Every route built by the routing models has to respect this limit.

  \item[Discharge Time:] The discharge time is the time needed to handle one parcel once the boat has docked. This thesis uses a fixed value of \qty{25}{\second} per parcel \parencite{toiparcellockers}. The total discharge time at a port is then the number of parcels delivered there, multiplied by \qty{25}{\second} and divided by the crew size, since the employees on board discharge the parcels in parallel. Together with the travel and maneuvering times, this discharge time makes up the total duration of a route.
\end{description}

Other constraints, for example handling time at the depot or travel time from the port to the locker, are not considered in this thesis, as they would require specific definitions of operational processes as well as precise port-to-locker distances. The selected constraints still provide a broad enough basis to build the model upon.
