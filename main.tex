\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

Global parcel volumes have increased substantially over the last decade, largely driven by e-commerce, and several industry forecasts expect continued growth toward 2030 \parencite{pitney_bowes_index_2023,wef2020}.At the same time, conventional truck-based delivery faces increasing operational constraints in many urban centres \parencite{lastmileimprove,su14084701}. Cities increasingly regulate freight access through measures such as low-emission zones, delivery time windows, and pedestrian areas, while dense or historic street networks can also limit the suitability of large vehicles \parencite{su151612266,MILENKOVIC2024101157,sustainableverlinde}. The last mile is therefore becoming both more constrained and more expensive at the very moment its volume reaches historic highs.

If trucks have less room to operate while parcels still need to reach customers, the question becomes which alternative delivery channel can fill this gap. One common response is to move to smaller vehicles such as electric vans and cargo bikes, which work well in dense urban areas where deliveries are clustered, but scale poorly to cities that are larger, hillier, or more spread out \parencite{NARAYANAN2022278}. Another answer predates these vehicles entirely. Cities have moved goods on water for most of their existence, and several are now reviving the practice \parencite{dhlboats,fludis}.

Located on Norway's western coast, Bergen combines a constrained urban road network with a geography shaped by fjords, making it a relevant candidate for such a system. This thesis therefore examines the implications of implementing a boat-based parcel delivery network in Bergen, in which selected shoreline ports would serve as parcel locker locations. Boats would operate along predefined routes between these ports to deliver parcels, while residents would collect their parcels by walking to their assigned nearest port.

\subsection{Problem Description}
\label{subsec:problem-description}

Designing a waterborne parcel network for a city like Bergen is not one isolated decision, but a sequence of connected choices. Three connected questions have to be answered together for the system to make sense as a whole.

The first question is where the boats should stop. Parcels arriving by water still need to reach the customers who ordered them, and this final step is completed on foot. The ports therefore have to be placed close enough to where people live, ensuring that the walking distance required to collect a parcel remains reasonable. Opening a port in every coastal neighbourhood would solve coverage but waste resources, while keeping only a few central ports would push too much walking onto the residents. The first part of the problem is to find a set of port locations that balances population coverage against the number of ports.

The second question is how the boats move between these ports. Once ports are chosen, each one carries a share of the city's parcel demand. The boats then have to leave the depot, visit the ports, drop the parcels and come back. The routes also have to respect realistic capacity and shift limits.

The third question is how many boats and crews are actually needed. A set of routes is not yet a fleet. The routes have to be packed into boats and shifts so that the network can operate every day of the year, including peak days when demand spikes. The fleet size drives the capital investment of the network, the crew count drives its labour cost, and both depend on the routes coming out of the previous stage.

These three problems are linked but not the same. Solving them in isolation would either produce an unrealistic fleet that ignores the city's geography, or a well-placed set of ports that no fleet can practically serve. This thesis treats them as a single chain where each stage takes the output of the previous one as its input.

\subsection{Research Question}
\label{subsec:research-question}

Building on the problem above, this thesis asks the following main research question:

\begin{quote}
  \textit{How can a waterborne parcel delivery network be designed for the city of Bergen, and what fleet and crew would such a network need to operate?}
\end{quote}

To make this question addressable, it is broken into three sub-questions, each one matching one step of the methodology:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item How many ports are needed, and where should they be placed, to cover the population of Bergen within a reasonable walking time?
  \item Given a set of selected ports and their parcel demand, what set of boat routes can serve them while respecting realistic capacity and shift limits?
  \item Given these routes, how many boats and crews are required to operate the network across low, average and peak demand days?
\end{enumerate}

Together, the answers to these three sub-questions give a complete picture of what the network would look like.

\subsection{Scope}
\label{subsec:scope}

This thesis focuses on the design of the network and its operational requirements rather than on a full real-world implementation. Several boundaries are drawn to keep the problem tractable.

On the demand side, port locations are defined using household locations only. As a result, the total network demand considered in the model is limited to household demand.

From an operational perspective, the depot location is selected manually based on its proximity to Bergen's existing port and rail infrastructure. Candidate port locations are also evaluated at a conceptual level only. They are considered suitable if they can potentially be served by boat, while a detailed site-level feasibility assessment is outside the scope of this thesis. Consequently, factors such as land ownership, operating rights, available quay space, water depth and handling capacity are not explicitly evaluated.

Several broader implementation considerations are also outside the scope of this thesis. Although the environmental impact of replacing trucks with boats is one of the main motivations for the topic, it is not quantified in the analysis. The financial cost of the network is also not modelled, due to the absence of reliable cost data for boat operation in this specific context. These aspects are instead highlighted as relevant directions for future work.

\label{subsec:thesis-structure}

The remainder of the thesis is organised as follows. Section~\ref{sec:background} reviews the relevant background, covering urban parcel delivery and the Bergen-specific context. \section{Background}

\label{sec:background}

\subsection{Urban Parcel Delivery and Last-Mile Logistics}
\label{subsec:urban-parcel-delivery}

With an expected \num{225} billion global parcels in 2028, up from \num{161} billion in 2022 \parencite{pitney_bowes_index_2023}, delivery companies will have to adapt quickly to a world that keeps ordering more parcels every year. Over long distances, capacity can still be added through more container ships, trains, and trucks. In city centres, however, there is only so much space for trucks. The supply chain can scale in response to rising demand, but its final bottleneck is simple: as goods move closer to customers, the vehicles serving each link get smaller \parencite{govuk2019lastmile}.

At the end of the chain, on what is called the \textit{last mile}, the job falls to medium and small trucks \parencite{govuk2019lastmile}. The last mile is widely recognised as the most expensive part of the supply chain, often accounting for more than half of the total delivery cost \parencite{savelsbergh2016}. It is also the most operationally complex. Routes have many short stops, vehicles are rarely full, and time windows are tight. Trucks also have to navigate dense city environments that are often poorly adapted to modern freight vehicles, especially in European historic centres where narrow streets, restricted access and limited road space make urban deliveries more difficult \parencite{BACHOFNER2022100020}. Combined with demand spread across the whole city, this forces trucks to be smaller, while they still need to make many deliveries to reach all customers, which in turn requires more trucks.

Meanwhile, cities that are often already experiencing record congestion from private car use are now also facing more delivery trucks on their roads \parencite{lindsey2023distributional}. Delivery vehicles contribute disproportionately to urban congestion relative to their share of total traffic \parencite{itf2022freight}. They are harder to electrify than cars, so they continue to generate high levels of CO2 and noise pollution while operating close to where people live \parencite{COULOMBEL2018514}. Even when they are electric, trucks are much heavier than passenger cars, which means more wear and tear on roads and higher levels of dust pollution \parencite{worldbank_lutp_curriculum}. Many of these costs are external to the carrier. The carrier pays for fuel and labour, while the city and its inhabitants pay for the rest \parencite{ec_internalisation_external_costs}.

In response, some cities and governments are creating projects and regulations to reduce these impacts. The research field that groups these efforts together is known as \textit{city logistics} \parencite{savelsbergh2016}. The goal is simple: replace one long, polluting last leg of delivery with a shorter, cleaner, and often shared one. This can be done in many ways.

One option is to set up micro-consolidation centres at the edge of dense areas, from which cargo bikes or other small electric vehicles can handle the last segment. If this is not feasible, truck traffic can be moved to off-peak hours to spread it out. Another option is to restrict high-emission vehicles from entering certain parts of the city, often the dense and walkable city centre \parencite{civitas2015}.

Finally, a solution that can create value for all parties is to consolidate household deliveries into small, shared pickup points across the city. This makes shipping companies more efficient since more demand is assigned to a single delivery spot, and it allows delivery trucks to serve only a few concentrated locations, often in commercial areas. For residents, this offers the option to pick up parcels when they are ready, rather than having to wait at home for delivery. In Norway, both PostNord and Posten have already rolled out automated parcel lockers along these lines, and they are now used by a large share of the population \parencite{toiparcellockers,posten_hours,portnord_hours}.

Many of these solutions involve the participation of both shipping companies and the population. The population plays a crucial role, because it often has to cover a portion of the delivery route itself. Even if a customer orders a package and would prefer to have it delivered to their address, the package may instead need to be picked up somewhere else, close to its original delivery point. In a perfect world, the customer would be able to make this pickup without causing any extra pollution. For this to work, the pickup points need to be reasonably close to where everyone lives. As more cities face the same challenge, a planning concept known as the \textit{15-minute city} has emerged \parencite{moreno2021}. The idea is simple. All essential services should be reachable within a \qty{15}{\minute} walk for every resident. This thesis adopts the same \qty{15}{\minute} threshold as the base walking time for its port selection model.

\subsection{Bergen as a Constrained Logistics Environment}
\label{subsec:bergen}

This thesis is based on the city of Bergen, located on the west coast of Norway. The city sits directly between the North Sea and the mountains that frame it. Bergen is famously known as the city of seven mountains, and these iconic peaks, combined with the fjords that cut deep into the urban area, leave only narrow strips of land where the population can live. People therefore live mostly close to the coast, while the land rises quickly into high mountain valleys further inland. The city is split into eight districts (\textit{bydeler}): Arna, Bergenhus, Fana, Fyllingsdalen, Laksevåg, Ytrebygda, Årstad and Åsane.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.5\textwidth]{maps/districts.pdf}
  \caption{Total population of Bergen in 2020 aggregated by district.}
  \label{fig:district_population}
\end{figure}

These districts can be seen geographically in Figure~\ref{fig:district_population}, and the full population breakdown by district is shown in the \nameref{sec:appendix} in Figure~\ref{anx:population-distribution}. They can be grouped into two sets. The first group, made up of Bergenhus, Årstad, Fyllingsdalen, Laksevåg and Ytrebygda, sits in the central and coastal part of the city, around the main fjords, and accounts for about \qty{65}{\percent} of Bergen's total population. The second group, Åsane, Arna and Fana, lies on the outer edges of the municipality. Because this second group has a much larger area with lower population density and mostly mountainous terrain, its road network is also less dense than in the first group.

Bergen also has significant elevation changes across its urban area, again caused by the surrounding mountains that force the population into limited coastal areas. Because flat coastal land runs out quickly, part of the population lives at higher elevations on the slopes leading up from the shore \parencite{ulaads_bergen,bergencasestudy}. This makes walking distance in Bergen harder to evaluate, as slope strongly affects walking speed. According to Tobler's Hiking Function \parencite{walkingstats}, a walk that takes 15 minutes on flat ground stretches to 21 minutes at a \qty{10}{\percent} incline. Walking is fastest at a \qty{5}{\percent} decline, where the same walk takes only 12.5 minutes, but steeper declines of \qty{10}{\percent} bring the pace back down to 15 minutes. Standard pedestrian network analysis tends to ignore elevation \parencite{noslope}, even though it is known to have a real effect on walking time in hilly cities like Bergen \parencite{slopegood}. Since the goal of this thesis is to explore the feasibility of a waterway shipping system rather than to plan a real operational schedule, slope is ignored in the walking time calculations and identified as a direction for future work.

Moreover, Bergen is known as one of Europe's rainiest cities \parencite{BREMER2020100236}. Frequent rain leads to wet pavements and infrastructure conditions that could make long walking distances less attractive, as does snow in winter. Nevertheless, like the slope, this limitation is ignored in this work, under the assumption that Bergen's residents are accustomed to local weather conditions. In a real-world scenario, it would be useful to compare the impact of weather on walking to port locations with its impact on driving.

The same geography that constrains road delivery offers a natural alternative. Because the population lives mostly along the coast, residents are already close to potential waterways. Roads, on the other hand, have to fight the geography. They either go around mountains and fjords, cross them with bridges, or cut through them with tunnels. Every workaround adds distance and creates a chokepoint. For boats, those same fjords are direct, open corridors between districts. What slows down road delivery is exactly what makes water delivery efficient.

Bergen also has the existing infrastructure to support such a system. As the second largest city in Norway, it hosts both the second largest port \parencite{bergenport} and the second largest cargo rail station \parencite{bergenrail} in the country, both located in the central area of the city. In practice, this means that the heavy logistics needed to feed a waterborne parcel system are already in place and would not need to be built from scratch.

\subsection{Waterborne Transport as an Urban Freight Alternative}
\label{subsec:waterborne-urban-freight}

While the trucking industry today dominates urban freight to the point where it can seem impossible to imagine cities working without it, large urban centres had to organise their cargo flows for centuries before trucks existed. For example, the Netherlands and England relied on their canal networks to move goods into and out of the city. Before mechanised land transport, waterways were central to freight movement, but this began to change with the rise of canals and railways during the Industrial Revolution \parencite{rodrigue2024emergence}. In recent years, several companies have started to revive this more traditional way of handling cargo. DHL is running waterway delivery trials in cities like Venice, Lagos and Berlin \parencite{dhlboats}, while Fludis is operating on the Seine in Paris \parencite{fludis}, both with the goal of reducing the number of trucks in city centres and the pollution that comes with them. The Paris case illustrates the approach well. The city is dense and difficult for trucks to navigate, which gave Fludis the opportunity to use the Seine as a backbone for its operations. The idea is simple. Parcels are first loaded with electric delivery bikes on a barge-like boat, the bikes are then loaded with parcels during the boat navigation, and the boat carries them upriver to a drop-off point. From there, the bikes handle the last-mile delivery and then return to the boat for the trip back to the main warehouse. This setup removes the short-range constraint of the bikes while keeping trucks out of the city itself.

Bergen has the right combination to do the same, and in some ways an even stronger one. Where Paris works with a single river, Bergen has a network of fjords and inlets that already touches almost every populated district. Using these waterways to reach the population by boat would free the streets of the polluting trucks that currently handle this work. A boat network would not be subject to peak-hour traffic, to the road bottlenecks created by Bergen's terrain, or to the geographic constraints that make some parts of the city hard to reach by road in the first place. On top of this, the fjords cut deep into the city in several places, which allows boats to reach areas that sit well inland from the open coast.

\section{Theory}
\label{sec:theory}

\subsection{Facility Location Problems}
\label{subsec:facility-location-problems}

The \ac{flp} family covers optimisation models that decide where to open facilities so that a set of demand points is served in the best possible way. The classical setup takes a set of candidate locations, a set of demand points, and a distance or cost for every demand-facility pair, and then asks which subset of locations should be opened. What counts as ``best'' depends on the objective, which usually balances the cost of opening facilities against the cost of serving demand from them.

The literature has converged on a small number of classical formulations. The \(p\)-median problem, introduced by \cite{hakimi1964optimum}, opens exactly \(p\) facilities and minimises the total weighted distance between demand points and their nearest facility. The \(p\)-center problem, also from \cite{hakimi1965optimum}, has the same structure but minimises the maximum distance instead of the total, which makes it useful when worst-case service matters. The \ac{sclp} of \cite{toregas1971location} takes a different angle. It fixes a coverage distance, considers a demand point covered if a facility is open within that distance, and minimises the number of open facilities under the constraint that every demand point is covered. The \ac{mclp} of \cite{church1974maximal} relaxes the requirement to cover everyone, fixes the number of facilities at \(p\), and maximises the demand covered within the distance threshold.

The \ac{psclp} sits between the \ac{sclp} and the \ac{mclp}. Like the \ac{sclp} it minimises the number of open facilities and uses a coverage distance to define what counts as served. Like the \ac{mclp} it accepts that not every demand point will be covered. The constraint is instead that a target share, or absolute amount, of demand must be covered \parencite{psclp}. The \ac{psclp} is therefore well suited to settings where full coverage is either too expensive or simply not realistic, and where the planner can specify a service level rather than a strict requirement.

Covering models are common in last-mile logistics, where deciding where to put pickup points, parcel lockers, or micro-depots is a central part of network design. \cite{schwerdfeger2020optimizing} use covering ideas to place mobile parcel lockers in urban areas, while \cite{deutsch2018parcel} study the location of pickup points under capacity constraints. The trade-off between the number of facilities and the share of customers within a comfortable walking distance is a recurring theme, which makes the partial covering family a natural fit \parencite{boyer2009last}.

\subsection{Vehicle Routing Problem}
\label{subsec:vehicle-routing-problem}

The \acf{vrp} family comes from a classic algorithmic problem, the \acf{tsp}. Its name already tells a lot about the challenge of solving it, as it consists of finding the minimum-distance route that reaches all nodes (customers) once while ending where it started \parencite{tps}.

The \ac{vrp} was first formulated by \cite{dantzig1959truck} as a generalisation of the \ac{tsp} to the case where a fleet of vehicles, instead of a single one, must serve a set of customers from a central depot. Since then the \ac{vrp} has grown into one of the most studied problems in combinatorial optimisation, with a large literature on both modelling and solution techniques \parencite{VIDAL2020401}.

Many variants of the \ac{vrp} have appeared over the years, each one adding features as it was used in new applications. For example, the \acf{cvrp} gives each vehicle a maximum load and forces the total demand on a route to stay below that limit. The \ac{vrptw} requires every customer to be visited within a given interval. The multi-depot \ac{vrp} allows several depots, the pickup and delivery \ac{vrp} combines both directions of flow, and the heterogeneous fleet \ac{vrp} works with vehicles of different types and capacities. A second distinction that matters for this thesis is between symmetric and asymmetric travel cost matrices. \ac{vrp} models may be formulated on either directed or undirected transportation networks \parencite{BRAEKERS2016300}. In this thesis, the waterway network is treated as symmetric, meaning that the travel time between two ports is assumed to be the same in both directions.

Moreover, if the constraint requiring each customer to be visited only once is too restrictive for the context, two approaches can be considered. The first approach is to relax this constraint directly. This leads to the \acf{sdvrp}, where vehicles keep the same capacity constraints as in the \ac{cvrp}, but customers may be visited more than once when their demand cannot be served by a single vehicle or route \parencite{ARCHETTI2011741}. The second approach is to keep the original \ac{cvrp} formulation and modify the input data instead, often called the \textit{priori split strategy} \parencite{a17090396}. In this case, a maximum demand limit is defined, and customers with demands above this limit are divided into several artificial customer nodes. Each clone represents part of the original customer demand and respects the imposed demand limit. These cloned customers keep the same distance values to all other customers, while the distance between clones of the same original customer is set to zero. This provides a practical simplification that allows a standard \ac{cvrp} model to approximate split deliveries without requiring a full \ac{scvrp} implementation. More specific splitting strategies can be used to further optimise the model, as uniformly splitting the demand could result in less optimal scenarios \parencite{a17090396}.

For this thesis, as the \ac{sdvrp} can get more expensive computationally \parencite{a17090396} and a lot of different configurations will be tested, but with only a relatively small number of ports and customer demands, the model chosen will be the \ac{scvrp} with the \textit{priori split} strategy.

\subsection{Vehicle Scheduling Problem}
\label{subsec:vehicle-scheduling-problem}

The \ac{vsp} usually appears after the routing decisions in transportation planning. In the standard planning hierarchy used in public transport and urban freight, decisions move from strategic choices about the network, to tactical choices about which routes to operate and how often, and finally to operational choices about which vehicle does which trip and when \parencite{ibarra2015planning}. Vehicle scheduling sits at the boundary between tactical and operational planning. Once the set of trips to run is known, the \ac{vsp} turns those trips into a usable schedule by matching them with the available fleet.

When the trips are fixed loops from depot to depot and the only question is how to assign these loops to vehicles within a fixed duration, the \ac{vsp} can be formulated as a bin packing problem. Each vehicle is represented as a bin with a capacity equal to the available working duration, and each loop is represented as an item with a size equal to its duration. The objective is to use the minimum number of vehicles needed to operate all loops.

In the context of this thesis, the number of routes to assign on a given day is expected to be small, while the number of available boats is not fixed in advance. The objective is therefore to determine the minimum number of boats required to operate all planned loops within the maximum working duration of each boat. For the main case study, a complete enumeration of all possible route-to-boat assignments would probably be sufficient. However, this approach does not scale well as the number of routes increases. Therefore, the problem is implemented as a bin packing optimization model, so that the same formulation can also be used in potential cases where a larger set of daily routes must be scheduled.

\section{Data and Operational Contraints}
\label{sec:data}

The data used in this thesis come from three independent sources, each addressing a distinct aspect of the problem: the spatial distribution of Bergen's population, the parcel demand it generates, and the pedestrian network connecting households to possible candidate ports. Population data is provided at the household level by Bergen municipality for the year 2020. Parcel demand is based on PostNord's weekly volumes by postal code in 2020. Finally, the walking network of Bergen is extracted from \ac{osm}. Additionally, this section outlines specific operational constraints. The remainder of this chapter describes these sources in detail, the steps taken to prepare them for use in the models that follow.

\subsection{Walking Network of Bergen}
\label{subsec:walking-network-bergen}

\ac{osm} is first used to retrieve the walking graph of Bergen with the Python package \texttt{OSMnx} \parencite{osmnx} and its \texttt{graph\_from\_place()} function. This package can retrieve the graph of a specific place (\texttt{place\_name=``Bergen, Norway''}) and network type (\texttt{network\_type=``walk''}). However, during spot checks of walking distances, the network type filter was found to be too narrow, as Google Maps walking distances were generally shorter than the distances calculated from the graph. After investigation, the network filter was found to exclude all bicycle lanes, even when they were labelled as walkable by \ac{osm}. The filter was therefore changed to allow bicycle lanes when they are walkable.

\subsection{Population and Household Data}
\label{subsec:population-household-data}

The household data was obtained from the Master's thesis of \cite{pop_thesis} and originally comes from Bergen municipality. The 2020 dataset contains information about the city's population at the household level. Only the coordinates, number of residents, postal code, and district were kept from the original data. The dataset contained \num{149308} households (\num{73393} unique), with a total population of around \num{280491}. This population is split across Bergen's eight districts and \num{142} unique postal codes.

To reduce the size of the data, the household coordinates were snapped to the nearest \ac{osm} node in the walking network. This reduced the dataset to \num{29991} unique \ac{osm} nodes. During this aggregation, some \ac{osm} nodes were assigned population from multiple districts, representing around \num{418} people (\qty{0.15}{\percent} of the population) and \num{27} nodes (\qty{0.07}{\percent} of nodes). Because some results are shown by district, these nodes were excluded. The population distribution by district can be seen in \Cref{fig:populations}. It should be noted that some districts will be filtered out of the population data in Section~\ref{subsubsec:walking-thresholds} (\nameref{subsubsec:walking-thresholds}).

\subsection{Distance and Travel Time Data}
\label{subsec:distance-travel-time-data}

Having defined the household-node and port-node datasets, the distance matrix can now be built. To achieve this, the Python package \texttt{networkx} \parencite{networkx} was used to calculate the shortest distance between all combinations of port and household nodes using its \texttt{single\_source\_dijkstra\_path\_length()} function. This resulted in an \(m \times n\) matrix \(D\), with \(d_{i,j}\) representing the Dijkstra shortest-path distance between the \(i\)-th port and the \(j\)-th household node\footnote{All pairs were reachable by walking due to the \ac{osm} node snapping and filtering of the port nodes.}. For this thesis, the distance between any \(port_i\) and \(household_j\) is assumed to be symmetric (\(d_{i,j} = d_{j,i}\)).

To calculate walking time, an average walking speed first needed to be defined. Based on the work of \cite{walkingspeed}, which reviewed numerous walking speed studies worldwide, a walking speed of \qty{1.25}{m/s} was identified as an appropriate general assumption. However, in the absence of studies specifically focused on Bergen, walking speed may differ from this general assumption and could be significantly affected by seasonal, weather, and topographical conditions, particularly elevation changes along walking paths. As the impact of these factors on walking speed is difficult to quantify specifically, the general assumption will be retained for this thesis. The resulting population coverage by walking time can be seen in Figure~\ref{fig:population_coverage} when all candidate port nodes are available.

\subsection{PostNord Parcel Demand Data}
\label{subsec:postnord-parcel-demand-data}

Parcel demand data was obtained from \cite{household}, but originated from PostNord. It covers weekly shipment and parcel volumes, from week \num{1} to week \num{48}, by postal code for the year 2020. The dataset had demand for \num{439} unique postal codes. It contained information about the number of shipments and parcels. However, in many cases, the number of shipments was larger than the number of parcels in a row. Demand was therefore calculated as the minimum of the two columns.

Unlike the household data, the real demand size is important for optimising port capacity. Because the PostNord dataset is from 2020, it needs to be adjusted to reflect 2026 levels. This was achieved using PostNord's annual reports, which give the total amount of parcel deliveries for each reporting year. PostNord's yearly demand can be found in Table~\ref{anx:yearly_pn_demand} in the \nameref{sec:appendix}. Based on these yearly demands, the 2026 Bergen demand can be derived using the \ac{cagr} formula. Due to the COVID-19 pandemic, only the years 2022 to 2025 are used to calculate the growth rate, starting from a year in which demand had begun to normalize after the pandemic \parencite{pitney_bowes_index_2023}. As shown in Equation~\ref{eq:postnord_cagr}, the rate is \qty{4.68}{\percent} per year. Therefore, 2026 demand is approximated as having grown by around \qty{48.56}{\percent} since 2020.

Second, because the dataset represents only PostNord's share of the market in 2020, it needs to be adjusted to current market figures. The \textit{Report on the Use of Postal Services by Private Individuals and Businesses in Norway, 2025}\footnote{Translated from \textit{Rapport om privatpersoners og bedrifters bruk av posttjenester i Norge - 2025} in Norwegian.} \parencite{nkom_post_2025}, published by the Norwegian Communications Authority, does not provide exact volume-based market shares for parcel deliveries, but it surveys consumer usage patterns across Norway's primary delivery companies. PostNord's market share was therefore derived from the normalized results of the private market parcel survey and estimated at around \qty{20}{\percent}. The survey data can be found in Table~\ref{anx:private_parcel_usage} in the \nameref{sec:appendix}.

Third, this dataset represents parcel demand for both households and other types of clients. However, since this thesis focuses on populations, the non-household demand needs to be removed. To achieve this, the household postcode data was joined with the weekly demand, and postcodes not present in the household dataset were excluded.

Finally, since some districts will be filtered out of the population data in Section~\ref{subsubsec:walking-thresholds} (\nameref{subsubsec:walking-thresholds}), the demand associated with these excluded household districts will also be removed. While several approaches were possible, such as splitting the demand equally across days or distributing it randomly, applying specific daily rates was chosen as the most representative of real demand patterns. Based on the work of \cite{dailydemand}, where daily parcel operations were studied, the 5-day split for this thesis context can be deduced by adding the weekend demand to Monday. The resulting daily rates can be seen in Table~\ref{anx:day_shares} in the \nameref{sec:appendix}.

\subsection{Disaggregating Postal Code Demand into Household Demand}
\label{subsec:postal-code-to-household-demand}

To correctly assign demand to the nearest chosen ports, the demand of each household must be known. However, the PostNord dataset is only available by postal code. Nevertheless, demand can still be allocated to each household based on its share of the population within its postal code. For example, if a postal code has a weekly demand of 100 parcels and contains two households, one with 1 resident (\(a\)) and the other with 2 (\(b\)), the demand is distributed as \(\frac{1}{3}\) to \(a\) and \(\frac{2}{3}\) to \(b\). It should be noted that this is a simplification for this thesis and is only used as an assumption. In addition, each household demand was rounded to an integer, resulting in a loss of \num{15.69} total parcels or \qty{0.000263}{\percent} of total demand.

\subsection{Depot Location}
\label{subsec:depot-location}

The depot is where incoming parcels are received, processed, and loaded onto the boats, so it needs enough space for this work and for docking the boats. However, these processing and handling spaces are considered only as context and are not quantified in this thesis. Furthermore, a good depot location should be close to the Port of Bergen and its cargo rail station, as both are the second largest of their kind in Norway \parencite{bergenport,bergenrail}. Finally, the depot also acts as a port itself, so a location with a high nearby population is the most efficient choice, as it removes the most parcels from boat transport.

\subsection{Operational Constraints}
\label{subsec:operational-constraints}

The models presented later in this thesis are based on a set of constraints that must be defined and justified before they can be implemented. The following section details each constraint and explains the values assigned to them. Some operational constraints, such as handling time at the depot or travel time between ports and lockers, are excluded from the scope of this thesis. Including these constraints would require a more detailed specification of the operational process and more precise port-to-locker distance data. Despite these exclusions, the selected constraints can still provide a reasonable foundation for the development of the models.

\subsubsection{Boat Maximum Capacity and Speed}
\label{subsubsec:boat-maximum-capacity-speed}

This thesis uses three scenarios to simulate different types of boats in the network, each representing a different total parcel capacity, from low to high. An average parcel weight of 4.5 kg is used as a modelling assumption. This value lies between the 4 kg average parcel weight reported by \textcite{stalhammar1996rating} and the 5 kg or lower average reported for most parcel operators in the Irish parcel delivery sector by \textcite{frontier2021ecommerce}.

The low-capacity scenario is based on the Candela P-12 Business boat \parencite{candela2026p12}. Even if this boat is not meant for cargo transportation, it is used as a representative vessel class to provide realistic characteristics for the models. Its service speed of \qty{25}{knots}, \num{30}-passenger capacity, and total payload limit of \qty{3000}{\kilogram} are taken directly from the manufacturer's specifications \parencite{candela2026p12}. To determine the parcel capacity, the passenger weight limit is used as a baseline. Assuming a standard passenger weight of \qty{75}{\kilogram} \parencite{passengerkg}, the payload dedicated strictly to parcels is \qty{2250}{\kilogram}. With an average parcel weight of \qty{4.5}{\kilogram}, this yields a total capacity of \num{500} parcels. The remaining \qty{750}{\kilogram} of the boat's total \qty{3000}{\kilogram} limit is then allocated to the handling materials required for these parcels. Dividing this remaining capacity by the \num{500} parcels yields a handling material weight of exactly \qty{1.5}{\kilogram} per parcel. This \qty{1.5}{\kilogram} per parcel ratio is used across the other scenarios to maintain a consistent proportion of handling weight to loading capacity.

The high-capacity scenario is based on the Kewatec Work 1920 \parencite{kewatec_work_1920}, a larger vessel made to transport cargo of many types. The total load capacity can be taken directly as \qty{15000}{\kilogram}, with a cruising speed of \qtyrange{25}{35}{knots}. With this capacity, the same average parcel weight of \qty{4.5}{\kilogram} per parcel, and \qty{1.5}{\kilogram} per parcel for handling, the total parcel capacity is exactly \num{2500} parcels.

The mid-capacity scenario is based on the Kewatec Work 1500 \parencite{kewatec_work_1500}, a vessel whose physical size sits directly between the small and large boat choices. Because specific payload data is not published by the manufacturer, a capacity of \num{1500} parcels is assumed to create a clean middle ground between the other two scenarios. As for the other capacity scenarios, the boat speed was specified by the manufacturer and is also \qtyrange{25}{35}{knots}.

For all scenarios, boat range is not taken into consideration in this thesis for simplification, as the routes will not be real optimized waterway navigation routes. It is therefore assumed that boats could stop to refuel or use strategically placed electric chargers at high-demand ports and the depot while unloading.

\subsubsection{Crew Size}
\label{subsubsec:crew-size}

Each boat is operated by a crew. The crew is responsible for discharging the parcels at the ports. Crew size will be tested with \num{2}, \num{3}, and \num{4} employees per boat. A minimum crew of two persons per boat is assumed, since single-handed operation is generally not recommended for small commercial workboats unless the vessel is specifically approved for it \parencite{mca2023workboatcode}. The maximum of 4 is set to have three practical scenarios.

\subsubsection{Maneuvering Time}
\label{subsubsec:maneuvering-time}

Every time the boat travels from one port to another, it needs time to dock and undock. This thesis accounts for it with a fixed maneuvering time, added to the travel time of every trip between two ports. The value used is a baseline of \qty{3.5}{\minute}, taken as the average of two estimates: a conservative one of about \qty{5}{\minute}, derived from a study of port operations on a passenger ferry \parencite{klovning2024efficiency}, and an optimistic one of \qty{2}{\minute}, based on the Candela P-12 \parencite{candela2026p12}.

\subsubsection{Discharge Time}
\label{subsubsec:discharge-time}

The discharge time is the time needed to handle one parcel once the boat has docked. This thesis uses a fixed value of \qty{25}{\second} per parcel \parencite{toiparcellockers}. The total discharge time at a port is then the number of parcels delivered there, multiplied by \qty{25}{\second} and divided by the crew size, since the employees on board discharge the parcels in parallel. Together with the travel and maneuvering times, this discharge time makes up the total duration of a route.

\subsubsection{Operating Hours}
\label{subsubsec:operating-hours}

To schedule the operations correctly, the operating hours must first be defined. Since this thesis focuses on Bergen, Norway, information from parcel service providers operating in the region, such as PostNord and Posten, can be used as a reference. Both providers offer parcel delivery services on weekdays, from Monday to Friday, with some exceptions made by Posten for certain types of parcels on Saturdays \parencite{posten_hours,portnord_hours}. Therefore, the operating days in this thesis are also set from Monday to Friday.

In addition, the daily operating hours must be specified. In this case, the Norwegian Working Environment Act provides a useful reference point. According to the Act, \enquote{Ordinary working hours must not exceed nine hours in a 24-hour period} \cite{arbeidsmiljoloven_10_4}. However, since this represents the maximum legal limit, a standard maximum of \qty{8}{\hour} per employee is assumed in this thesis. Therefore, any given boat route, from the moment it leaves the depot to the moment it returns, cannot last longer than \qty{8}{\hour}. This upper bound represents a normal working shift for the crew. The overall operation is nevertheless modeled over a \qty{24}{\hour} planning horizon, allowing several employees or shifts to cover the full day if required.

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:methodology}

In this section, the methodology of this thesis is explained in detail, in three separate parts. First, the model used to select a set of ports based on a desired walking distance is presented. Second, the model used to generate a set of routes for these ports is described. Finally, it is explained how, given a set of ports and routes, a schedule can be constructed.

\subsection{Port Selection Model}
\label{subsec:port-selection-model}

The goal of this stage is to find the smallest set of ports that still covers a target share of the population within an acceptable walking time. The selection depends on two inputs: the walking threshold and the target coverage percentage. These two configurations are presented first, followed by the optimisation model chosen to perform the selection.

\subsubsection{Walking Thresholds}
\label{subsubsec:walking-thresholds}

As discussed in the background section, a walking time of \qty{15}{minutes} is often a target when planning new city programs for residents. The walking time coverage distribution was already shown in Figure~\ref{fig:population_coverage}, but it is also important to show the same data by district. This can be visualized in Figure~\ref{fig:district_coverage}.

The districts can be visually grouped into three categories based on how their population coverage evolves with the walking time threshold. The first group, composed of Bergenhus ({\color[HTML]{FF7500}\rule{1.5ex}{1.5ex}}), Ytrebygda ({\color[HTML]{7F7F7F}\rule{1.5ex}{1.5ex}}), and Laksevåg ({\color[HTML]{955348}\rule{1.5ex}{1.5ex}}), shows a rapid increase in coverage, reaching near-full coverage within approximately \qtyrange{35}{40}{\minute} of walking. The walking time threshold considered for this problem ranges from \qtyrange{15}{30}{\minute}, with every integer value within this range tested. Values below \qty{15}{\minute} were judged as too low for this context, as the resulting coverage was very limited, while values above \qty{30}{\minute} were judged as impractical, since citizens are unlikely to walk such distances to collect a parcel.

\subsubsection{Coverage Percentages}
\label{subsubsec:coverage-percentages}

As achieving \qty{100}{\percent} coverage for a given walking time threshold could require a disproportionately large number of boats, due to certain households only being reachable through specific ports, a minimum coverage percentage will instead be defined to address this issue. It was decided that the range would be \qtyrange{75}{100}{\percent}, with every percentage point within this range tested. This range should be large enough to show if some ports are added only for a small portion of the population.

  \min \quad & \sum_{i \in I} f_i y_i \label{eq:psclp-objective}                                        \\
  \text{s.t.} \quad
             & \sum_{i \in I(j)} y_i \geq z_j
             &                                                   & j \in J \label{eq:psclp-coverage}    \\
             & \sum_{j \in J} d_j z_j \geq D
  \label{eq:psclp-demand}                                                                               \\
             & y_i = 1
             &                                                   & i \in P \label{eq:psclp-preselected} \\
             & y_i \in \{0,1\}
             &                                                   & i \in I \label{eq:psclp-y-binary}    \\
             & z_j \in \{0,1\}
             &                                                   & j \in J \label{eq:psclp-z-binary}
\end{align}

The objective function~\eqref{eq:psclp-objective} traditionally minimizes the total fixed cost of the opened ports. Since every candidate port is given the same opening cost (\(f_i = 1\)), this is the same as minimizing the number of ports selected for the network.

Constraint~\eqref{eq:psclp-coverage} ensures that for a household node \(j\) to be covered (\(z_j = 1\)), at least one port from its subset \(I(j)\) must be opened. If no port able to reach \(j\) within the walking threshold \(T_{\max}\) is opened, \(z_j\) is forced to remain uncovered (\(z_j = 0\)).

Constraint~\eqref{eq:psclp-demand} ensures that the covered set of household populations is greater than or equal to the minimum covered population \(D\).

Constraint~\eqref{eq:psclp-preselected} forces every preselected port to be opened. The depot is part of this set, as the boats operate out of it and it must therefore always be among the selected ports (Section~\ref{subsec:depot-location}).

The last two constraints,~\eqref{eq:psclp-y-binary} and~\eqref{eq:psclp-z-binary}, keep the port and coverage variables binary.

\subsubsection{Model Implementation}
\label{subsubsec:psclp-implementation}

The \ac{psclp} is implemented with Google OR-Tools \parencite{ortools}, using its constraint-programming solver CP-SAT \parencite{cpsat}. One adjustment was needed to fit the model to the solver. CP-SAT only works with integer coefficients, but the coverage target \(D = \alpha \sum_{j \in J} d_j\) is usually not a whole number, since \(\alpha\) is a fraction. To account for this, the population and the coverage target are multiplied by \num{10000} and rounded to integers before the demand constraint~\eqref{eq:psclp-demand} is built. This keeps three decimal places of precision, so the rounding has no meaningful effect on the result.

The model is kept as compact as possible by removing, before the optimization, all households that cannot be covered by any candidate port within the walking threshold \(T_{\max}\). These households are therefore excluded from the formulation, and no associated coverage variable \(z_j\) is introduced.

\subsubsection{Scenario Selection}
\label{subsubsec:scenario-selection}

Each pair \((T_{\max}, \alpha)\) produces one feasible \ac{psclp} solution, but carrying every combination through the routing and scheduling steps would be impractical. A subset is therefore selected using a utopia-point compromise rule. Following the logic of the Euclidean compromise solution, each run is compared to an ideal point that contains the most favourable value for each criterion. The coverage \(\alpha\), the number of opened ports, and the average weighted walking time are min-max normalised across the relevant set of runs. The kept run is the one with the shortest Euclidean distance to the ideal point \((1, 0, 0)\), representing maximum coverage, minimum number of ports, and minimum walking time \parencite{voorneveld2011euclidean}.

This rule is applied twice. First, for each walking threshold \(T_{\max}\), the best run among the feasible \(\alpha\) values is kept. The run best run for \(T_{\max} = \qty{15}{\minute}\) will be chosen as the first scenario as it is of particular interest as it matches the ``15-minute city'' reference discussed in the background section. Second, a single overall best run is identified across all \((T_{\max}, \alpha)\) pairs. These selected scenarios are then carried forward to the boat routing models presented in the next section.

\subsection{Boat Routing Models}
\label{subsec:boat-routing-models}

\subsubsection{Demand Allocation to Selected Ports}
\label{subsubsec:demand-allocation-to-selected-ports}

The \ac{psclp} gives a set of selected ports, but it does not specify which households each port should serve. Each household is assigned to the nearest selected port based on the distance matrix \(M\). The total household demand is then summed by port to obtain weekly demand for each port.

It should be noted that this is done for every household, not only for the ones the \ac{psclp} counted as covered. A household that was left uncovered, meaning that no selected port can be reached within the walking threshold \(T_{\max}\), is still assigned to its nearest selected port, only with a longer walk. The selected ports therefore receive the parcel demand of every household in the included districts, and the coverage threshold \(\alpha\) only shapes which ports are selected and how far people have to walk, not the amount of demand the network has to carry. This decision was made to keep all household demand because a project like this would require an assumed large amount of capital and cooperation between shipping companies and the city of Bergen. Therefore, keeping as much demand as possible was assumed to be the best option.

\subsubsection{Port Distance Matrix}
\label{subsubsec:port-distance-matrix}

Although the computation differs from the household-to-port distance matrix, the same matrix-based logic is used here. A separate port-to-port distance matrix is constructed to represent the travel distances between each pair of selected ports. However, in complex environments like the fjords in Bergen, calculating true maritime distances between close ports is difficult. Standard Euclidean distances are inadequate, as linking two ports with a straight line would probably go straight through a mountain and ignore the complex, narrow waterways of the real boat route.

Although more advanced methods exist to calculate maritime distances around coastline constraints, implementing a full shortest-path model for Bergen's coastline is outside the scope of this thesis. Given that the \ac{psclp} reduces the problem to a relatively small set of selected ports, it was decided to use a simpler manual approach instead. This is considered sufficient for the objective of the thesis, which is to estimate network-level boat travel times rather than to provide exact navigation routes or operational sailing instructions.

The construction is done in two steps. The first step is done manually in QGIS. The selected ports are first grouped into a small number of visual zones based on their geographic position. The relevant distances are then drawn directly on top of the \ac{osm} basemap as polylines that follow a plausible boat path between two ports, and the length of each polyline is taken as the distance in nautical mile. Three sets of arcs are then drawn. The first connects every pair of ports located within the same zone. The second connects each pair of zone polygons using a single edge-to-edge polyline. The third connects each port to the depot. Since the objective is to estimate network-level travel times, inter-zone movements are approximated through representative edge-to-edge connections rather than by computing every possible port-to-port distance directly. The \qty{5}{knots} and \qty{8}{knots} speed-restricted segments introduced in the background section are also included at this stage. For each polyline, the portions crossing these restricted zones are identified and assigned the corresponding travel speed.

The second step is done in code and fills in the arcs that were not drawn by hand. The directly measured arcs from the first step are loaded as the edges of a weighted graph over the selected ports. For every port pair that has no direct measurement, the shortest path through the graph is computed with Dijkstra's algorithm and used as an estimate of the missing distance. An overhead correction of 0.35 nmi\footnote{This value was estimated from a small set of manual checks, by drawing polylines between selected ports and the nearest existing route. It is therefore used as an approximate correction factor rather than an exact navigational distance.} is applied to each intermediate stop on the path to account for the fact that a boat going directly between two ports would not have to detour through every port on the way. The output is a complete port-to-port distance matrix, combining the directly measured arcs from the first step and the graph-based estimates from the second one, which is the matrix consumed by the routing models.

\subsubsection{Demand Scenarios}
\label{subsubsec:demand-scenarios}

The routing models do not run on the raw daily port demand, but on three representative daily demand scenarios per port: a Low, an Average and a Peak scenario. Working with discrete scenarios rather than the full daily series keeps the number of routing runs manageable while still covering a quiet, a typical and a busy day, which is enough to test whether the fleet sized for an average day can absorb seasonal swings.

The three scenarios are anchored on three reference months. For every month of the year, the median of total daily demand is calculated, and the months are ranked by this median. The month with the lowest median is taken as the \textit{low month}, the one in the middle of the ranking as the \textit{average month}, and the one with the highest median as the \textit{peak month}. Using months instead of individual days smooths out single-day noise, like holidays or weather events, while keeping the seasonal signal.

Once the three reference months are fixed, each scenario is built at the port level. The Low scenario takes, for each port, the median of its daily demand observed during the low month, which corresponds to a typical quiet day. The Average scenario does the same on the average month and represents a typical day on which the network should comfortably operate. The Peak scenario takes the 95\textsuperscript{th} percentile of the port's daily demand observed during the peak month. The 95\textsuperscript{th} percentile is preferred to the maximum to represent a realistically busy day without sizing the fleet on a single extreme outlier. Each scenario value is rounded up to the next integer so that the routing models treat demand as whole parcels.

\subsubsection{Model Implementation}
\label{subsubsec:scvrp-implementation}

The first routing model used is the \ac{scvrp}, as it is a natural fit for this problem: the depot is fixed, each selected port has a known weekly parcel demand, and the fleet is composed of identical boats with a maximum parcel capacity and a maximum daily working time. The objective is to design a set of routes, each starting and ending at the depot, that together serve all port demands while minimising the total travel time of the fleet.

Several configurations were tested for the \ac{scvrp}, each combining a boat capacity, a crew size and a weekly demand scenario, summarised in Table~\ref{tab:cvrp_configs}. The three boat scenarios and their parcel capacity are defined in Section~\ref{subsubsec:boat-maximum-capacity-speed}, the three crew sizes in Section~\ref{subsubsec:crew-size}, and the three demand scenarios in Section~\ref{subsec:port-demands}. The boat cruising speed is fixed across all configurations at \qty{25}{knots}, which is the common achievable speed of every vessel considered in Section~\ref{subsubsec:boat-maximum-capacity-speed}.

The classic \ac{scvrp} mathematical model is not defined in this thesis, as it was also not explicitly defined in the code. The objective minimises the total travel time of the fleet, where the cost of every arc is the sum of the boat travel time \(t_{ij}\) between the two ports and the manoeuvring time \(m\). Parcel handling time is left out of the optimization function because it is constant, as all demand is handled at the same speed regardless of the route. To allow split deliveries while keeping the standard \ac{scvrp} structure, where each stop is visited by exactly one vehicle, the demand of every port is decomposed into multiple service nodes that share the port's coordinates, each one carrying at most \num{10} parcels\footnote{The threshold of \num{10} parcels was chosen after sensitivity testing. In the tested scenarios, increasing the maximum service-node demand beyond this value did not improve the routing solutions.}. The routing model treats these service nodes as independent stops but with no travel cost between them, making it possible for the same port to be visited several times by different routes.

The handling time at port \(i\) is \(d_i \tau / e\), with \(d_i\) the parcel demand of the port for the run, \(\tau\) the per-parcel discharge time from Section~\ref{subsubsec:discharge-time}, and \(e\) the crew size of the configuration. It represents an estimate of the time needed to put the parcels into the lockers at the port. As mentioned above, the travel time between the port and the locker is not taken into account in this thesis.

Two capacity constraints are added to the solver. The first corresponds to the maximum parcel capacity of the boat in the current configuration, denoted by \(Q\). This constraint ensures that the total demand assigned to a route does not exceed the boat capacity. The total demand is calculated by summing the demand covered by the boat at each port visited in the current route.

The second constraint corresponds to the maximum route duration, denoted by \(T^{r}_{\max}\). This constraint represents an operational restriction requiring employees to complete their route before the end of their shift. The total route duration includes travel time, manoeuvring time, and handling time at each visited port.

The search is configured with \texttt{PARALLEL\_CHEAPEST\_INSERTION} as the first-solution strategy and \texttt{GUIDED\_LOCAL\_SEARCH} as the local-search metaheuristic \parencite{ortools_routing}, with a time limit of \qty{1}{\hour} per run. This combination produces a feasible solution quickly and then iteratively improves it; the best solution found within the time limit is retained.

\subsection{Boat Scheduling Model}
\label{subsubsec:boat-scheduling-optimization-model}

The \ac{scvrp} gives the set of routes that the fleet needs to run on a given day, but not how many boats and crews are needed to run them. The boat scheduling model fills this gap by assigning each route to a boat and deciding how many crew shifts each boat has to run.

A simple bin packing model is enough at this stage. The routes from the \ac{scvrp} already start and end at the depot and respect the maximum route duration, so two routes on the same boat can be run one after the other with no scheduling conflict. The network also does not commit to specific delivery times at the ports, so no time windows have to be enforced. The model therefore only has to decide which routes share a boat and how many shifts each boat needs. The boat count is minimised first and the total shift count second, since the boat count sets the capital cost of the network and the shift count sets the labour cost.

Each route is treated as a single fixed-duration, indivisible task equal to its total route time. Boats are interchangeable, and a single boat can run several routes during the day, as long as the total work fits within the \qty{24}{\hour} planning horizon from Section~\ref{subsubsec:operating-hours}. A single crew, however, is limited to one shift of at most \qty{8}{\hour}, so a boat that works more than eight hours in the day must operate over several shifts, each shift covered by a new crew. Because a route cannot be split across shifts, the model assigns every route to a specific shift of a specific boat, and not only to a boat. Table~\ref{tab:scheduling_notation} summarises the notation used in the formulation.

    [3ex]

    [3ex]
    \(z_{ks}\)      & \(\begin{cases} 1, & \text{if shift } s \text{ of boat } k \text{ is used} \\ 0, & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}\)                 & Binary \(\in \{0, 1\}\) \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabularx}

The problem is formulated as follows:

\begin{align}
  \min \quad & M \sum_{k \in K} u_k + \sum_{k \in K} \sum_{s = 1}^{\bar{n}} z_{ks} \label{eq:sched-objective}                                                                                 \\
  \text{s.t.} \quad
             & \sum_{k \in K} \sum_{s = 1}^{\bar{n}} y_{rks} = 1                                              &  & r \in R \label{eq:sched-assign}                                            \\
             & \sum_{r \in R} \sum_{s = 1}^{\bar{n}} \delta_r \, y_{rks} \leq H \, u_k                        &  & k \in K \label{eq:sched-day}                                               \\
             & \sum_{r \in R} \delta_r \, y_{rks} \leq S \, z_{ks}                                            &  & k \in K,\ s \in \{1, \ldots, \bar{n}\} \label{eq:sched-shift}              \\
             & z_{ks} \leq u_k                                                                                &  & k \in K,\ s \in \{1, \ldots, \bar{n}\} \label{eq:sched-link}               \\
             & u_k \geq u_{k+1}                                                                               &  & k \in K \setminus \{|K|\} \label{eq:sched-symmetry-boat}                   \\
             & z_{ks} \geq z_{k,\,s+1}                                                                        &  & k \in K,\ s \in \{1, \ldots, \bar{n} - 1\} \label{eq:sched-symmetry-shift} \\
             & y_{rks} \in \{0,1\}                                                                            &  & r \in R,\ k \in K,\ s \in \{1, \ldots, \bar{n}\} \label{eq:sched-y-binary} \\
             & u_k \in \{0,1\}                                                                                &  & k \in K \label{eq:sched-u-binary}                                          \\
             & z_{ks} \in \{0,1\}                                                                             &  & k \in K,\ s \in \{1, \ldots, \bar{n}\} \label{eq:sched-z-binary}
\end{align}

The objective function~\eqref{eq:sched-objective} minimises the number of boats used first and the total number of shifts second. The fleet size is prioritised because each additional boat is a capital investment, while a shift only adds a crew for the day. The weight \(M\) on the boat count is set well above the maximum possible total shift count \(|K| \cdot \bar{n}\), so the solver can never trade a shift saving for an extra boat.

Constraint~\eqref{eq:sched-assign} forces every route to be assigned to exactly one (boat, shift) pair.

Constraint~\eqref{eq:sched-day} limits the total workload of boat \(k\) at the operating day length \(H\) when the boat is used, and forces it to zero otherwise.

Constraint~\eqref{eq:sched-shift} ensures that the sum of route durations placed in shift \(s\) of boat \(k\) must fit within the maximum shift length \(S\). This per-shift constraint is what guarantees feasibility at the crew level, since routes are indivisible and cannot be split across shifts.

Constraint~\eqref{eq:sched-link} prevents a shift from being used unless its boat is also marked as used, keeping the boat count \(\sum_k u_k\) consistent with the shift assignment.

Constraints~\eqref{eq:sched-symmetry-boat} and~\eqref{eq:sched-symmetry-shift} break symmetry by forcing boats and the shifts within each boat to be used in increasing index order. Without them, the solver would explore equivalent permutations of the same assignment, which slows the search considerably.

The last three constraints,~\eqref{eq:sched-y-binary},~\eqref{eq:sched-u-binary} and~\eqref{eq:sched-z-binary}, keep the decision variables in their respective domains.

Once the model is solved, the optimised schedule can be rebuilt from the decision variables to form a route-to-boat-and-shift assignment, with a total working time and a shift count \(\sum_s z_{ks}\) for each boat \(k\). As with the \ac{psclp}, the model is implemented with Google OR-Tools \parencite{ortools} using its CP-SAT solver \parencite{cpsat}. Route durations \(\delta_r\) are converted from hours to integer seconds before being passed to the solver, since CP-SAT only accepts integer coefficients. A solver time limit of \qty{2}{\minute} per run is set, which is well above what is needed as the number of routes per day stays low and an optimal solution is returned in under a second.

It stays within a small range between 19.68 and \qty{21.92}{\minute} across the selected runs, with a standard deviation below one minute, even though \(T_{\max}\) doubles from \qtyrange{15}{30}{\minute} and the number of ports drops from 28 to 17. Reducing the number of ports by \num{11} therefore costs the average citizen around two minutes of additional walking. This suggests that the floor of the average walking time is set by Bergen's geography rather than by the strictness of the model. Further analysis could determine whether new port locations could be added or constructed to help reduce this metric.

\subsection{Demand Scenarios}
\label{subsec:demand-scenarios}

As discussed, three demand scenarios were chosen to test the waterway network based on possible daily demands. The full year of daily demand and the three scenarios are shown in Figure~\ref{fig:yearly_demand}, where each day is coloured according to the scenario that could handle its demand.

Two patterns stand out. First, a within-week variation is visible, with the highest demand on Monday, a dip in the middle of the week, and a partial recovery by Friday. This pattern is not observed in the raw data but comes from the daily demand shares introduced in Section~\ref{subsec:weekly-to-daily-demand}, which spread the weekly total unevenly across the five weekdays. Second, a clear seasonality can be seen across the year. Demand rises sharply in November and December, most likely driven by the Christmas period in Norway, while noticeable drops appear in April and May, and again in late July. January stands out the most, with very low demand compared to the rest of the year. This could be the result of missing data in the PostNord dataset, although the weekdays of January are indeed present for the entire month. Another explanation could be the post-Christmas slowdown, when consumer and retail activity tend to drop at the start of the new year.

The low-demand scenario corresponds to a daily network demand of \qty{10732}{parcels}. The peak scenario is more than four times higher at \qty{44405}{parcels}, while the average scenario sits in between with \qty{21518}{parcels}. As shown in Figure~\ref{fig:yearly_demand}, very few days in the observed year fall within the low-demand range, with most of them concentrated at the very beginning of January. The low scenario therefore serves more as a reference for the quiet end of the demand range than as a typical operating day. By contrast, the average and peak scenarios bound the majority of the actual year, with most weekdays falling between the two thresholds.

\subsection{Boat Routings}
\label{subsec:boat-routing-results}

The \ac{scvrp} model returns many results to discuss. These are grouped by the selected \ac{psclp} solution they use (\texttt{t15} or \texttt{t28}) and by configuration (see Table~\ref{tab:cvrp_configs}). Each selected solution has \num{27} results. All configurations were run for \qty{1}{hour} to make sure that the solver had enough time to find a good solution. Each run resulted in a successful solution, although none were proven optimal by the solver. Most runs found their best solution in a matter of seconds, after which the rest of the run duration achieved nothing. Others, such as \texttt{t15} with (\(Q=2500, e=4)\) and (\(Q=1500, e=3)\), successfully found better solutions after around \qty{50}{\minute}. An interesting observation is that the configurations able to take advantage of the \qty{1}{\hour} run time are not the same in both \texttt{t15} and \texttt{t28}. In \texttt{t28}, the configurations with \(Q=500\) were the last to find their best solutions. In hindsight, the fact that some scenarios took this amount of time could be a clear indication that the \ac{sdvrp} could have been worth modeling instead of the simpler \ac{scvrp}, because the model clearly took a lot of time to find better solutions.

\subsubsection{Port Distances}
\label{subsubsec:used-port-distances}

As explained in \nameref{subsubsec:port-distance-matrix}, some port-to-port distances were not measured directly, but were estimated through intermediate graph paths. This approximation remains acceptable for the routing analysis, not because the estimated distances are assumed to be exact, but because the optimized solutions relied only marginally on them. Across all tested scenarios, more than \qty{95}{\percent} of the distances actually used by the models corresponded to real manually measured polylines. The results are therefore mainly driven by directly measured maritime distances, while the graph-based estimates only serve to complete the matrix when no direct measurement was available.

\subsubsection{Total Number of Routes}
\label{subsubsec:total-number-routes}

Figure~\ref{fig:total_routes_and_median_capacity} shows the number of routes needed for each configuration, together with the median capacity utilization across all routes.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering
  % First image
  \includegraphics[width=0.49\textwidth]{python/graphs/routing/t15_a095_routing_capacity_utilization.pdf}
  \hfill
  % Second image
  \includegraphics[width=0.49\textwidth]{python/graphs/routing/t28_a098_routing_capacity_utilization.pdf}

  % The single shared caption
  \caption{Number of routes required under the low, average, and peak demand scenarios, and corresponding average capacity utilization, for each configuration and demand scenario.}
  \label{fig:total_routes_and_median_capacity}
\end{figure}

The first observation is straightforward: the number of routes is inversely correlated with boat parcel capacity. As expected, the less a boat can carry on a given route, the more routes it has to make to serve the demand.

The second observation is that the number of routes is not strongly affected by the number of employees (\(e\)) in either \texttt{t15} or \texttt{t28}. Only \(Q=2500\) makes a difference, with both \(e = \{3,4\}\) requiring fewer routes than \(e=2\). At first, this could look like an error, as more employees mean faster parcel handling, which should result in shorter routes and potentially different best solutions. However, the solver's objective is not to minimise the number of routes, but to minimize the total travel time for a given day. As a result, parcel handling time is not taken into consideration by the objective function, which explains why the number of routes is mostly unaffected in these results. Only with the largest boat capacity, \(Q=2500\), does the reduction in handling time make enough of a difference for the solver to find a different routing network.

The third observation is that median capacity utilization varies considerably depending on boat capacity (\(Q\)). For \(Q=2500\), in both \texttt{t15} and \texttt{t28}, median utilization is lower than for the other capacities. The lowest median, at \(e=2\), suggests that with only two employees, demand has to be split into more routes because handling time is too large. Route capacity is therefore limited not by the boat capacity, but by the speed of parcel handling. Adding one more employee (\(e=3\)) greatly improves the median, bringing it above \qty{90}{\percent} for all demand scenarios. However, the improvement stops at \(e=3\), with \(e=4\), the median is the same per demand scenario as with the previous \(e\) for \texttt{t28} and stays around \qty{95}{\percent} for \texttt{t15}. This may indicate that \(Q=2500\) is too large for this context, as adding more employees has little impact on median capacity utilization. The exact opposite can be seen with \(Q=500\), where median capacity utilization sits around \qtyrange{95}{100}{\percent} in all configurations and scenarios, signalling that capacity is a clear constraint because the routes are often almost full. Although a \qty{100}{\percent} capacity utilization rate may appear optimal in theory, it reduces operational flexibility and limits the system's ability to respond to variations in demand.

In general, it could be argued that because \(Q=2500\) has the worst capacity utilization, its capacity is too large and the routes are mostly limited by the total route duration constraint of \qty{8}{\hour}, not by the boat's capacity. The same logic can be applied in reverse to \(Q=500\). Since its median capacity utilization is always very high, and the number of routes needed is very large compared to the other capacities, this capacity may be too small for the demand. However, because \(Q=2500\) has few routes, its medians need to be interpreted with caution, while \(Q=500\), with many more routes, is more reliable.

\subsubsection{Total Route Duration}
\label{subsubsec:total-route-duration}

The next metric is the total travel time of the routes. In Figure~\ref{fig:total_and_median_routing_time}, these durations are shown by run and configuration, with a visual split between total navigation time and parcel handling time. Lines representing the median route time in hours are also present for each demand scenario.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering
  % First image
  \includegraphics[width=0.49\textwidth]{python/graphs/routing/t15_a095_routing_travel_time.pdf}
  \hfill
  % Second image
  \includegraphics[width=0.49\textwidth]{python/graphs/routing/t28_a098_routing_travel_time.pdf}

  % The single shared caption
  \caption{Total route time by configuration and demand scenario, separated into navigation time and parcel handling time.}
  \label{fig:total_and_median_routing_time}
\end{figure}

The first observation is clear: as with the total number of routes needed, total route time increases across all configurations as demand scenarios rise. This observation, paired with the seasonality seen in Figure~\ref{fig:yearly_demand}, shows how much the system would have to expand to handle peak demand. However, in these periods, the number of employees per boat could be increased to help reduce overall route time.

The second observation is that, compared with the results from Figure~\ref{fig:total_routes_and_median_capacity}, where the total number of routes did not really change when \(e\) changed, the real effect can now be seen. For example, as seen in \texttt{t15}, all employee counts had the same number of routes per demand scenario. However, it can be seen here that even though the number of routes is equal, total route duration is smaller.

A third observation is that the reduction in total route time is not directly proportional to the number of employees added to the crew. For example, in \texttt{t28}, going from \(e=2\) to \(e=3\) reduced the average total route time by \qty{26}{\percent}, while going from \(e=3\) to \(e=4\) only achieved a reduction of \qty{17}{\percent}. This diminishing return follows directly from the model, since only the parcel handling portion of the total time scales with \(e\). The handling time at a port is \(d_i \tau / e\), while the navigation and manoeuvring times do not depend on \(e\) at all. The same pattern also explains why the reduction is stronger at higher boat capacities, where each route carries more parcels and the handling share of the total time is larger, leaving more work for the additional crew member to absorb.

A fourth observation is that the reduction in total route time from increasing the boat capacity \(Q\) is also not linear. Going from \(Q=500\) to \(Q=1500\) reduced the average total route time by \qty{29}{\percent}, while going from \(Q=1500\) to \(Q=2500\) only achieved a reduction of \qty{7}{\percent}. This is consistent with the capacity utilisation results in Figure~\ref{fig:total_routes_and_median_capacity}, which already showed that the largest boats were not filled to their full capacity. The extra space offered by \(Q=2500\) over \(Q=1500\) is therefore mostly unused and brings little additional benefit on the total route time.

A final observation can be made about median route duration. Looking at \(Q=500\) reveals that the routes are very short, only \qtyrange{2}{3}{\hour}, while the maximum constraint is set at \qty{8}{\hour}. This shows that with this capacity, the routes are limited not by travel time but by boat capacity, which further supports the capacity utilization results in Figure~\ref{fig:total_routes_and_median_capacity}. The opposite can be observed for \(Q=2500\).

To summarise, the total route time follows a clear pattern of diminishing returns for both the crew size \(e\) and the boat capacity \(Q\). The parcel handling dominates the total time and is the only component that responds to a larger crew, while the navigation time stays fixed regardless of the configuration. Each new employee, or each step up in capacity, therefore contributes less than the previous one, and the largest boats remain partly empty. Combined with the strong seasonal variation of the demand, this suggests that the most efficient strategy is a balance rather than a maximum, with the crew size potentially adjusted between low and peak periods instead of permanently oversizing the fleet.

\subsubsection{Total Route Distances}
\label{subsubsec:total-route-distances}

The next metric is total route distance in nautical miles. This is important because, in a real scenario, boats have a maximum range and need to be refuelled or recharged. However, as previously stated, the model does not have any constraints on maximum route distance, only on duration and total capacity. The value for this metric can be seen in Figure~\ref{fig:total_and_median_distances}.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering
  % First image
  \includegraphics[width=0.49\textwidth]{python/graphs/routing/t15_a095_total_distance_per_run.pdf}
  \hfill
  % Second image
  \includegraphics[width=0.49\textwidth]{python/graphs/routing/t28_a098_total_distance_per_run.pdf}

  % The single shared caption
  \caption{Total sailing distance and average distance per route for each configuration and demand scenario.}
  \label{fig:total_and_median_distances}
\end{figure}

The first observation is that total distance drops drastically between \(Q=500\) and \(Q=1500\). This is explained by the decrease in the total number of routes, as seen in Figure~\ref{fig:total_routes_and_median_capacity}. More routes for the same total demand mean more navigation to and from the depot, which serves no demand and rapidly increases distance. Having more capacity therefore reduces the number of routes, which tends to reduce total travelled distance. In contrast, as with the other metrics discussed previously, the changes from \(e=2\) to \(e=3\) are much larger than those from \(e=3\) to \(e=4\). This reinforces the assumption that \(Q=500\) may be too small, while \(Q=2500\) may be too large.

The second observation is that the median distance per route behaves differently across scenarios. For average and peak demand, it stays between \qtyrange{21}{22}{nmi} for \(Q \in \{500, 1500\}\) in both runs. At \(Q=2500\), the average climbs fast while the peak keeps a slow and steady rise. In contrast, the low demand scenario is less predictable. At \(Q=1500\) its median stays flat across all values of \(e\), around \qty{26}{nmi} in \texttt{t15} and \qty{24}{nmi} in \texttt{t28}. This suggests that the typical route distance is relatively stable regardless of \(e\). At \(Q=2500\), \texttt{t15} and \texttt{t28} diverge. For low demand, \texttt{t15} drops to about \qty{23}{nmi}, while \texttt{t28} climbs to a peak of more than \qty{28}{nmi} at \(e=2\) before falling back to around \qty{26}{nmi}. The average scenario also splits. In \texttt{t15} it peaks at \qty{25.7}{nmi} at \(e=3\) and drops slightly at \(e=4\), while in \texttt{t28} it peaks at a much lower \qty{25}{nmi} for \(e=3\) and stays mostly untouched for \(e=4\). Most of this variation comes from the small number of routes at \(Q=2500\). With only around \qty{5}{routes}, removing a single one of \qty{27}{nmi} between \(e=2\) and \(e=3\) is enough to shift the median by several nautical miles.

To summarise, capacity is the main driver of total distance. The biggest drop happens between \(Q=500\) and \(Q=1500\), while pushing further to \(Q=2500\) brings smaller gains. The number of employees \(e\) barely changes anything at \(Q=500\) and \(Q=1500\) since the model minimizes navigation time and not distance, and only starts to matter at \(Q=2500\), where the small number of routes also makes the median per route sensitive to small changes.

\subsubsection{Boats Needed}
\label{subsubsec:boats-needed}

The first observation concerns the low number of boats needed compared to the number of routes from Figure~\ref{fig:total_routes_and_median_capacity}. For example, with \(Q=500\) at peak demand, all \num{76} routes are covered by only \num{10} boats. This works because the routes are very short, so many of them can be chained on the same boat within the \qty{24}{\hour} daily window. As routes get longer, fewer of them fit on a single boat. The variation in boat count between demand scenarios stays high, although it shrinks when more employees are assigned per boat, since a larger crew shortens each route.

The second observation is that the number of boats needed across \(Q\) values within the same demand scenario stays similar for both low and average demand scenarios. Indeed, the standard deviation stays below \num{1}, with values between \numrange{1}{3} boats for low demand and \numrange{2}{5} boats for average demand. The peak scenario shows more variation, with the boat count ranging from \num{5} to \num{10} and a standard deviation of about \num{1.8}. On top of that, both \texttt{t15} and \texttt{t28} require a very similar number of boats, which means the capital cost of the fleet cannot really be used to decide between the two routing scenarios. Moving from average to peak demand, however, increases the boats needed by \qty{95}{\percent} on average, which confirms that demand level is a real driver of fleet size.

The final observation for the boats needed is that the number of employees per boat still plays a role in reducing the boat count in higher-demand scenarios, while reducing it by only \num{1} in the low-demand scenario. This still represents around \qtyrange{33}{50}{\percent}, depending on boat capacity. Nevertheless, for all scenarios, even if the smallest crew size is used for the low-demand scenario and the highest for the peak scenario, the number of boats would still have to at least double to serve all demand scenarios for a given \(Q\).

\subsubsection{Employees Needed}
\label{subsubsec:employees-needed}

This metric helps identify the impact that different values of \(e\) have on the total employee count for the day. The first observation is that increasing \(e\) almost always increases the total number of employees needed, with one exception at \(Q=1500\). For this boat capacity, in every demand scenario, going from \(e=2\) to \(e=3\) raises the employee count, but going from \(e=3\) to \(e=4\) lowers it by an average of \qty{22}{\percent}, while also reducing the number of boats needed. As shown in Figure~\ref{fig:total_routes_and_median_capacity}, the total number of routes did not change with different values of \(e\) for this \(Q\). However, Figure~\ref{fig:total_and_median_routing_time} showed that both the total and the median routing time decreased. This means that the extra employees made the routes shorter, which allowed the same crew to cover more routes during the day, and therefore reduced the total employee count.

For the other values of \(Q\), the behaviour is more straightforward. Going from \(e=2\) to \(e=3\) increases the total employees needed by an average of \qty{20}{\percent}, while reducing the number of boats by \qty{18}{\percent} on average for \(Q=500\) and \qty{7}{\percent} for \(Q=2500\). For \(Q=2500\), the boat reduction only happens between \(e=2\) and \(e=3\) and disappears between \(e=3\) and \(e=4\). This shows that even when adding more employees makes the routes shorter, the gain is not always large enough to free up an entire boat.

In a real-world setting, this tradeoff between fleet size and crew size could be settled on a cost basis, as long as the daily operating cost of a boat and the daily cost of an employee are both known. Without those figures, only assumptions can be made here, and the best configuration stays dependent on the cost structure a future operator works with.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

\subsection{Answer to the Research Question}
\label{subsec:summary-of-findings}

With the results now defined, the research question asked at the beginning can now be answered in three parts.

\begin{quote}
  \textit{How can a waterborne parcel delivery network be designed for the city of Bergen, and what fleet and crew would such a network need to operate?}
\end{quote}

\begin{enumerate}
  \item How many ports are needed, and where should they be placed, to cover the population of Bergen within a reasonable walking time?
\end{enumerate}

Two reasonable walking times were chosen, 15 and 28 minutes. For the lowest, it was found that 28 ports could cover \qty{95}{\percent} of the population within the walking reach, and 20 ports for \qty{98}{\percent} of its reachable population. The first scenario demonstrates a good walking time target to achieve a more plausible distance, while the latter serves as a compromise between the number of ports and the walking distance. However, the results indicate that both port scenarios lead to similar average walking times to the nearest port. The \qty{15}{\minute} scenario has an average walking time of \qty{19.68}{\minute}, compared with \qty{20.73}{\minute} for the \qty{28}{\minute} scenario. This suggests that, in practice, the difference between the two networks is smaller than the \(T_{\max}\) values alone might suggest.

\begin{enumerate}[resume]
  \item Given a set of selected ports and their parcel demand, what set of boat routes can serve them while respecting realistic capacity and shift limits?
\end{enumerate}

Three demand scenarios were tested, representing a low, average and peak demand period. For both port scenarios, the number of routes was similar; the real driver was the demand. The number of routes ranges from only 4 to 18 for the low demand depending on the capacities, 8 to 37 for the average and 15 to 76 for the peak.

\begin{enumerate}[resume]
  \item Given these routes, how many boats and crews are required to operate the network across low, average and peak demand days?
\end{enumerate}

For both walking distance scenarios, the number of boats and employees is extremely similar, varying again mostly based on the demand scenarios. For low demand, the fleet ranges from 1 to 3 boats, with 10 to 20 employees. For the average demand, the fleet ranges from 2 to 5 boats with 20 to 36 employees. Finally, for the peak demand, the fleet would have to be from 5 to 10 boats with 38 to 76 employees.


In conclusion, \texttt{t15} and \texttt{t28} ended within a few percent of each other on every metric, which means the two layouts would be similar to operate. The results, however, show the extent to which the operations would have to scale to accommodate the low and the peak demand. Furthermore, the results showed that the boat capacity of 1500 parcels would be more efficient compared to the low and high capacities, offering more flexibility than the 500 capacity with a better overall capacity utilization. For the crew size, 3 employees helped reduce the number of boats needed, while increasing the size to 4 showed great results in peak demand scenarios.

\subsection{Further Research}
\label{subsec:further-research}

The most direct extension is to add cost data. With a daily cost per boat, per employee, and a fixed cost per port, the choice between \texttt{t15}, \texttt{t28}, and the \((Q, e)\) configurations becomes a simple optimisation on top of the existing results.

A second direction is to refine the walking time by accounting for elevation. Bergen is hilly, and a slope corrected travel time over the full round trip to the port would likely change which ports the \ac{psclp} selects, especially in the steeper districts.

A third direction would be to extend the model to a heterogeneous fleet setting, where boats with different parcel capacities can be used in the same solution. This could improve the routing results, since larger boats may be useful for high-demand ports, while smaller boats may be better suited to lower-demand ports. A combination of capacities could therefore create a more efficient network by matching boat size more closely to port demand.

A fourth direction is to validate the port candidates on the ground, since some of them may be private, too shallow, or otherwise unsuitable in ways that open data cannot detect.

Finally, the environmental side of the project, which motivated this thesis but was kept outside its analytical scope, can now be quantified using the route distances, durations and fleet sizes produced here, and compared against the emissions of the boats themselves.
